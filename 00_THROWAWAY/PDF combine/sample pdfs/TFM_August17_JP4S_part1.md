Stick to the ‘no-brainer’ buys, 
however scarce they are

Most people have no idea how to define quality and figure out the 
value of the products on offer. This leads them to buy all the stuff 
regardless of the real underlying quality and valuation. All they 
need is the label ‘dividend payer’ stamped on the packaging and 
maybe some price reduction compared to yesterday’s price.

I’ve read an analysis recently which stated that the hunt 
for yield had caused dividend stocks to reach valuation

Let me illustrate this point with an easy-to-understand 
analogy. Imagine that the stock market itself is a 
shopping mall in which every store represents a certain 
category of stocks. Most people have the feeling that 
this particular mall is so expensive these days that it is 
not worth visiting at all. (Notice the analogy: the stock 
market as a whole looks overvalued from a historical 
perspective.)

In the meantime, the FALCON is circling above the horde 
of these people objectively scanning the field of targets 
from the distance without any emotional biases. Suc-
cessful investing requires that you have a (preferably ev-
idence-based) process and you stick to it through thick 
and thin.  Sounds simple? Well, investing is really simple 
but it isn’t easy. Putting together a process like the 
FALCON Method is not the hardest part. The consistent 
application is where the real difficulties arise. Investing 
involves emotions and they have to be managed. Fear 
and greed are all over the place, while patience and dis-
cipline are rare. You need to resist the former and excel 
in the latter to become a successful long-term investor. 
Easier said than done.

Let’s say that you venture into this mall anyway and you 
have the feeling that all the quality stuff you would be 
interested in buying look way overpriced so your wallet 
should be kept shut. You routinely walk to your favorite 
store inside the mall (the one that represents the group 
of dividend stocks) only to see a sign that says “We 
have doubled the prices on every item”. Although the 
sign effectively tries to scare people away you notice 
that a huge crowd of resolute wannabe shoppers is still 
circling this store. Strange as this may seem you keep 
observing the ‘show’ to see that whenever some items 
go on sale in this particular store the offers only last for 
a few moments as someone scoops up the cut price 
merchandise within seconds.

What I do instead is have a look at the stock price in 
relation to various measures of value (like adjusted 
operating earnings, operating cash flow, free cash flow, 
EBITDA) and compare the current valuation ratios to the 
historical averages of different time frames. Notice that 
while the dividend yield can also be a good thermometer 
of value within a carefully selected group of stocks with 
immaculate dividend histories, this indicator can badly 
mislead the investor if examined in a vacuum. Whatever 
you do valuation remains an art but the more angles you 
look at the same picture from the more details you may 
notice and the more confident you can become about 
your opinion.

OK, wait! That part on valuation begs for some further 
explanation. You may have heard the general notion that 
the value of any asset stems from the amount of cash 
it can put into the investor’s pocket. Think about rent-
al property or a simple bond to get the point. The first 
problem with stocks is that you need to define which 
cash category you consider the basis of valuation. Is it 
the dividend that you truly receive or is it the free cash 
flow that the management is free to use (hopefully) for 
value creation purposes?

One school of thought says that stocks paying no 
dividends at all are like fancy baseball cards or other col-
lectors’ items the value of which is entirely dependent 
on what other people are willing to pay for them. If there 
are no dividends you really need a willing and able buyer 
to realize any value on your investment. (Just as a side-
note: I do consider the free cash flow category utterly 
important although I admit that investing in ‘no dividend 
stocks’ based on their free cash flow trends alone can 
make the investor extremely vulnerable to Mr. Market’s 
mood swings since in this case price is the only compo-
nent of your total return.)

With enough experience, you will have a feeling that the 
particular stock under examination is a ‘no-brainer’ buy 
or rather a ‘pass’. In the current market environment, 
there are not too many no-brainer buys within our ‘Pre-
mium Dividend Club’ of stocks. Remember that we are 
looking at the over-popular store in that shopping mall 
with all the people circling around it and they are much 
less picky than we are, to put it mildly.

Now you’ve got a glimpse of today’s market reality but 
the main point is to draw the right conclusions. We are 
not going to switch stores and start hunting for replica 
products of subpar quality. Neither will we loosen our 
valuation criteria. Stick to the plan instead of forcing 
things! Our plan involves buying dividend stocks at 
historically undervalued levels so that we get paid to 
wait while Mr. Market changes his opinion on valuation 
thus giving us an exceptional total return. This is a low 
stress, low activity way of investing that lets you live 
a meaningful life. Don’t worry, no market goes up or 
down forever. At some point, the correction will come 
and good values will be available.  All we need to do is 
recognize and acquire them. Patience, discipline and 
a good process form the holy trinity of investing. The 
FALCON Method provides the latter, while the other two 
must come from you. Now, let’s see what the FALCON 
spots this time!

Opinion

Opinion
Lowe’s is operating in a market where smaller competitors have much less bargaining power with suppliers and are 
unable to match the broad assortment of inventory and in-store product presentations that this company can afford. 
What is this if not a moat? The dominant player in the segment is Home Depot which might be the reason why the 
market underappreciates Lowe’s stock. In fact consumers have few reasons not to head to Lowe’s or Home Depot 
for their home improvement needs and if the competition were that intense, neither of these companies would be 
in a position to increase prices and still enjoy healthy growth in transactions. There is no Amazon disruption here, 
Lowe’s is spending nearly half of the company-wide capital expenditures on its online omni-channel sales platform. 
Housing trends are supportive at the moment but the company’s ability to hit its growth targets is largely tied to the 
health of the overall economy, and specifically the construction industry. Interest rate increases can be a headwind 
and I consider the numerous acquisitions risky. Combined with its margin expansion plans and the aggressive share 
buybacks, Lowe’s believes it can achieve 15% annual EPS growth in the coming years. According to management’s 
35% dividend payout ratio target, this should result in 15% to 20% annual dividend growth. I foresee an annualized 
total return of 12-14% if the growth materializes and the valuation multiple stays the same. The latest quarterly fig-
ures will be released on Wednesday, Aug 23. (Disclosure: I have bought a small position in the stock recently.)

Glossary

Adjusted funds from operations (AFFO): This calculation takes a REIT’s FFO (see later) and subtracts any recurring 
expenditure that is capitalized and then amortized, as well as any straight-lining of rents.

Blended P/E: A price-to-earnings indicator, where the earnings part is the calculated as the weighted average of the 
most recent actual reported earnings plus the closest quarterly forecast earnings. This gives the most weight to the 
past “actual” reported earnings, but also includes an appropriate consideration of the company’s continuing earnings 
power post their last report.

CEO: Chief Executive Officer

CFO: Chief Financial Officer

CROIC: Cash Return On Invested Capital. This indicator shows how much free cash flow per dollar the business 
generates from invested capital. This can be the ultimate performance metric as it shows so clearly how effective 
management is and the strength of the business. (CROIC = FCF/Invested Capital)

Dividend yield: The percentage of the price per share a company pays out to its shareholders as dividends annually, 
calculated by dividing the forecasted 12-month dividend payout by the current price.

DGR: or Dividend Growth Rate is the compound annual growth rate of the dividend for the periods shown.

EBITDA: Earnings before interest, taxes, depreciation, and amortization is essentially net income with these things 
added back to it. It is used to analyze and compare profitability without the effects of finance and accounting deci-
sions.

EVP: Executive Vice President

Ex-Dividend Date: If you purchase a stock on its ex-dividend date or after, you will not receive the next dividend pay-
ment. Instead, the seller gets the dividend. If you purchase before the ex-dividend date, you get the dividend.

FCF payout ratio: This alternative dividend payout ratio aims to be more accurate by excluding accounting earnings 
and including only actual cash generation. It is calculated as Dividend Per Share as a percent of Free Cash Flow per 
Share.

FCF yield: Free Cash Flow per Share for the trailing 12-months divided by the current stock price. This is the inverse 
of the Price to Free Cash Flow (PFCF) value.

Free cash flow (FCF): Free Cash Flow shows how much cash a company generates after paying to maintain and 
expand its production. It is calculated by subtracting Capital Expenditure (Cap Ex) from Cash Flow.

Funds from operations (FFO): This refers to the figure used by real estate investment trusts (REITs) to define the 
cash flow from their operations. It is calculated by adding depreciation and amortization to earnings, subtracting any 
gains on sales. Many leading REITs believe that the FFO calculation is the best presentation of operating results. 
This is because real estate historically rises and falls with macroeconomic conditions, and any operating results 
calculated using the cost accounting method are not an accurate measure of performance.

Payout Ratio: Dividend payout ratio is Dividend Per Share as a percent of Diluted Earnings Per Share and can be

ROIC: ROIC, or Return on Invested Capital, quantifies how well a company generates cash flow relative to the capital

Shareholder yield: Shareholder yield is the total of share buybacks and dividend payments to common shareholders

ny is profiting more from issuing new stock than it is spending on buybacks and dividends.

Closing Thoughts

After having read the opening section how do you think something like Bitcoin can be valued? If an asset puts no 
cash into your pocket during the holding period and has no history of being priced in correlation with an underlying 
metric (like its dividends or cash flow) then how do you know whether the current price represents a bargain or a 
bubble? Thoughtful investment decisions can only be made if you have a good sense of value. Everything else is 
speculation or pure future telling based on some price charts.

While I have a really good sense of when quality dividend payers go on sale, I have absolutely no idea about the 
intrinsic value of speculative assets (if they have any). I am only confident in investing my money when I know that 
I’m buying a top quality asset (with decades of immaculate dividend history) at cheaper than usual valuation (with a 
margin of safety) and I am collecting cash payments (dividends) while waiting for Mr. Market to revalue my stocks.

I always have an estimate of my potential total-return at the time of purchase. This is really no big deal: the total 
return is influenced by dividends, growth, and changes in the valuation multiple. You can come up with a decent esti-
mate for any reasonable investment. How about a speculative thing like Bitcoin? Seriously, how would you work out 
a ballpark total return estimate? Looking at the steepness of the price chart? Honestly, there’s not much I myself 
could do since the tools of valuation I am comfortable using are simply useless here and without any proven method 
of valuation, I am simply not willing to part with my money. I am an investor, not a gambler.
