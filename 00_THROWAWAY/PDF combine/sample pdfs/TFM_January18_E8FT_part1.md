Should Be

“Many people consider value investing dull and as boring as 
watching paint dry. As a consequence value investors are not 
always listened to, especially in a stock market bubble. Investors 
are often in too much of a hurry to latch on to growth stocks to 
stop and listen because they’re afraid of being left out. Very often it 
would be a good thing if they were.” (Peter Cundill)

2017 was a difficult year for value investors yet it was 
as pleasant and smooth as it can get for everyone else 
in the stock market. The broad market averages kept 
moving higher on almost a daily basis while only a hand-
ful of high-quality stocks were available at fair or better 
valuations. When the sensible (and picky) investor has 
so few stocks to pick from watching everyone else profit 
and boast can be really hard. This is the time when Mr. 
Market asks the question: are you ready to abandon 
your discipline and play “me too”, or do you stick to 
what you know works over the long-term?

As to the essence of the slow and steady approach, let 
me illustrate my point with an example I have read in 
one of Nassim Nicholas Taleb’s books. Imagine we have 
Kevin working as a dentist and John playing Russian 
roulette with a bored billionaire. Very different lives, 
indeed! Kevin knows that a dentist will never get rich in 
a single day but he can do very well “over thirty years of 
motivated, diligent, disciplined, and regular attendance 
to teeth-drilling sessions”. His friend, John, on the other 
hand, wins $10 million every time the revolver fancies 
him. Should Kevin envy John’s lucky millions? For a 
thoughtful person like him, it is obvious that John’s prof-
its are simply cash borrowed from destiny with some 
random payback time. Anytime they meet John will be 
well ahead financially, so he can boast about his fortune 
until the day he is dead.

The most wonderful thing about the stock market is 
that this is your choice. You can decide for yourself if 
you want instant gratification, with the risk of losing big 
or stay disciplined and take the slow and steady course 
to build a pool of capital that can provide a stream of 
dividend income now and into the future.

Most people in John’s category are involved in the 
greater fool game meaning that they are buying some-
thing of questionable value with the sole intention of 
selling it to someone who is willing to pay even more 
for it. Short-term results can be eye-popping but such 
stories seldom end well. (Human history is filled with 
examples of us temporarily believing things that later 
turn out to be total nonsense. This was my side note to 
the ones involved in the cryptocurrency mania.)

comes to mind when the head of the U.S. patent office 
resigned because he deemed that there was nothing left 
to discover. We have no clue about the future and my 
best advice for you is to hold no idea about how things 
should be! This way you can experience freedom like

Glossary

Adjusted funds from operations (AFFO): This calculation takes a REIT’s FFO (see later) and subtracts any recurring 
expenditure that is capitalized and then amortized, as well as any straight-lining of rents.

Blended P/E: A price-to-earnings indicator, where the earnings part is the calculated as the weighted average of the 
most recent actual reported earnings plus the closest quarterly forecast earnings. This gives the most weight to the 
past “actual” reported earnings, but also includes an appropriate consideration of the company’s continuing earnings 
power post their last report.

CEO: Chief Executive Officer

CFO: Chief Financial Officer

CROIC: Cash Return On Invested Capital. This indicator shows how much free cash flow per dollar the business 
generates from invested capital. This can be the ultimate performance metric as it shows so clearly how effective 
management is and the strength of the business. (CROIC = FCF/Invested Capital)

Dividend yield: The percentage of the price per share a company pays out to its shareholders as dividends annually, 
calculated by dividing the forecasted 12-month dividend payout by the current price.

DGR: or Dividend Growth Rate is the compound annual growth rate of the dividend for the periods shown.

EBITDA: Earnings before interest, taxes, depreciation, and amortization is essentially net income with these things 
added back to it. It is used to analyze and compare profitability without the effects of finance and accounting deci-
sions.

EVP: Executive Vice President

Ex-Dividend Date: If you purchase a stock on its ex-dividend date or after, you will not receive the next dividend pay-
ment. Instead, the seller gets the dividend. If you purchase before the ex-dividend date, you get the dividend.

FCF payout ratio: This alternative dividend payout ratio aims to be more accurate by excluding accounting earnings 
and including only actual cash generation. It is calculated as Dividend Per Share as a percent of Free Cash Flow per 
Share.

FCF yield: Free Cash Flow per Share for the trailing 12-months divided by the current stock price. This is the inverse 
of the Price to Free Cash Flow (PFCF) value.

Free cash flow (FCF): Free Cash Flow shows how much cash a company generates after paying to maintain and 
expand its production. It is calculated by subtracting Capital Expenditure (Cap Ex) from Cash Flow.

Funds from operations (FFO): This refers to the figure used by real estate investment trusts (REITs) to define the 
cash flow from their operations. It is calculated by adding depreciation and amortization to earnings, subtracting any 
gains on sales. Many leading REITs believe that the FFO calculation is the best presentation of operating results. 
This is because real estate historically rises and falls with macroeconomic conditions, and any operating results 
calculated using the cost accounting method are not an accurate measure of performance.

OCF: Operating cash flow. The measure of cash into or out of the company, similar to Net Income but less easily 
manipulated and without the effects of depreciation and other non-cash charges.

Payout Ratio: Dividend payout ratio is Dividend Per Share as a percent of Diluted Earnings Per Share and can be 
used to measure the chance of a dividend increase or cut. For example, a company with a small Payout Ratio has 
room to increase its dividend.

ROIC: ROIC, or Return on Invested Capital, quantifies how well a company generates cash flow relative to the capital 
it has invested in its business. It is defined as Net Income / (Total Equity + Long-term Debt and Capital Lease Obliga-
tion + Short-term Debt and Capital Lease Obligation)

Shareholder yield: Shareholder yield is the total of share buybacks and dividend payments to common shareholders 
over the past twelve months as a percent of the current market capitalization. A negative value indicates the compa-
ny is profiting more from issuing new stock than it is spending on buybacks and dividends.

TTM: trailing twelve months.

How to use the newsletter?

If you are saving and investing regularly, it is a wise strategy to buy the highest ranked stock in the Top10 every 
month in which you have the smallest dollar amount invested, or go for the Top Pick of the month if you do not own 
it yet. (I prefer the latter.) With this approach, you can build a well-diversified portfolio of top quality stocks pur-
chased at attractive prices.

If you have a lump sum to invest I suggest that you should divide the money into 24 equal parts and invest it grad-
ually during the next 24 months (1/24 of the money each month), always purchasing the most attractive stocks on 
the Top10 list of the FALCON Method Newsletter. This way you can free yourself from the frustration of the mission 
impossible called market timing.

You may also have your own criteria, like “the dividend yield must be above 3%”. In this case, pick the stocks from 
the Top10 that fulfill your requirements, and do this every month. Although if you notice that none of today’s best 
opportunities can jump over your bar, it may be time to think whether your expectations are still realistic in the pre-
vailing market environment.

My suggestion is to go for 20-30 stock positions and make them approximately equal weighted. This way you will 
reap the rewards of diversification, while you can have a portfolio of top companies from various sectors and these 
stocks will put ever increasing dividends in your pocket.

If you start following the recommendations of the FALCON Method Newsletter, after a few years you might have 
more positions than you feel comfortable with. In this case, I recommend that you sell the stock in your portfolio 
that ranks the lowest in the complete ranking and use the proceeds to buy one of the Top10 stocks in which you 
own the least dollar amount.

Closing Thoughts

Dethroning CVS Health begs for some explanation as the stock would still occupy the first place on our list if I hadn’t 
removed it manually. The company is acquiring Aetna in a blockbuster deal the details of which are hard to like. I 
sent out an email alert to subscribers on December 5 so I will only focus on the reasons behind the stock’s relega-
tion this time.

CVS is purchasing a seemingly overvalued company and is financing the transaction both by issuing more of its 
undervalued shares and taking on a huge amount of debt. The company will keep its dividend flat and stop the share 
repurchase program until they pay down some of the debt. Crunching the numbers makes me think that they will 
not raise the dividend for at least 2-3 years. While the combined company may have huge potential the short-term 
effects of the transaction are negative. A dividend yield below 3% coupled with a freeze and no repurchase activ-
ity would relegate the stock from our Top 10 as the trailing 12-month data gets updated to the worse quarter by 
quarter. Why wait for this to happen when CVS provided us with a clear picture of the near future?

Now that CVS Health gave up its place in the Top 10 to a new company, I must emphasize that CVS stock is not a 
sell! We have a 3-step recipe for success: 1. We buy ownership stakes in quality companies when they look un-
dervalued. 2. We hold their shares patiently until the market recognizes the true value. 3. We sell when a stock gets 
overvalued or we are proven unmistakably wrong by a dividend cut. We have done everything right with CVS in the 
first step and none of our selling rules got triggered so we are in the “hold the shares patiently” phase. Knowing 
what I know now I wouldn’t buy CVS today but there’s no way I would sell my shares at the current price either. This 
position has all the potential to turn out to be a great long-term holding.

Now that I’m done with the explanation part let me give you a little insight on how our Top 10 list is constructed in 
today’s extreme market. This has been the most challenging issue of the newsletter to write so far. We have 341 
stocks that meet our criteria on dividend history. 56 of them could be categorized as “undervalued or fairly valued” 
so were eligible for further analysis. Only 17 met all of our absolute threshold criteria. You read it right: in the current 
market environment by the time we get to implement our multifactor quantitative ranking tool we are down to 17 
stocks! Once I remove CVS and others with far too volatile earnings and cash flow histories there is hardly anything 
left to rank. Yet in this market, I keep getting emails like “Give me an additional stock to buy”. Come on, think about 
it!

The major dilemma among thoughtful investors today is whether to stick with the ever-narrowing group of under-
valued stocks or we should venture to pay more for businesses of somewhat better quality. With the latter, your 
downside protection is given by their growth potential and not their current valuation. However, growth needs time 
to materialize and if a stock market correction comes sooner than this, you may be in for a memorable experience. 
As no one knows the exact timing of the next correction or outright bear market there is no correct answer to which 
course the investor should take. (We’ll only know in hindsight.) For my own part, I’m on the cautious side and limit 
myself to the undervalued stocks as long as that breed is not completely extinct.

Being a beginner or inexperienced investor in today’s market can be really dangerous unless one follows a systemat-
ic approach with discipline. Be patient, stick to the plan and enjoy every minute of the journey!
