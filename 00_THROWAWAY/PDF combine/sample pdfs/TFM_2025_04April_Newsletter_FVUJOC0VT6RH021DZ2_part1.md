is clearly reflected in our model portfolios. Juggernauts

precision, whereas laggards such as Walgreens and 
Advance Auto Parts continue to underperform. Endersen 
further emphasizes, “Some of my biggest losses have

from an active value investing approach toward EVA

higher bar to pass for inclusion.

SABM #4: “High ROIC Cookie Cutters.” These are 
highly predictable reinvestment machines—some of

franchising model. Wingstop, Starbucks, Chipotle, and

SABM #9: “Brands.” Durable brands hold an advan-
taged position in customers’ minds, which translates 
into loyalty and pricing power. Brands help consumers 
feel good about themselves, creating an emotional con-
nection that reduces the cognitive load of decision-mak-
ing. Apple, Nike, Starbucks, and luxury names such as 
Hermès and Ferrari fall into this category. That said, 
developing and maintaining this moat source requires 
consistent investment and management attention.

SABM #5: “User-Generated Content.” This category 
includes traditional platform businesses that receive 
content for free, making them increasingly valuable 
to both consumers and advertisers. Classic examples 
include Meta’s social networks (Facebook and Insta-
gram), Microsoft’s LinkedIn, and Alphabet’s YouTube 
and Waze.

Last but not least, SABM #10: “Ecosystem Enablers.” 
Such businesses are genuinely win-win, as the value 
they extract from the ecosystem is a mere fraction of 
the value they create for users. Alphabet is a prime ex-
ample—consider Google Workspace (Sheets, Calendar, 
Gmail) or Maps, which are provided for free but have 
become indispensable to many of us.

SABM #6: “Both Sides Now.” This label refers to 
two-sided networks, which often lead to a winner-takes-
all share of the relevant profit pool. Once both sides are 
scaled, the barriers to entry become very high. Examples 
include social networks like Tencent and Meta, search 
engines such as Google, and e-commerce platforms like 
Amazon. Payment networks like Visa and Mastercard 
also fit this model. From our EVA Monster watchlist, Air-
bnb and Booking benefit from this same effect, making 
them worthy candidates for a deep-dive analysis.

tended to work out very well.” This is precisely what we 
aim for with our EVA Monster strategy. Now, let’s see 
what the FALCON spots this time!

Evolution AB (EVVTY)

Company Overview (Sector: Consumer Cyclical / Gambling)

Evolution AB develops, produces, and licenses live casino and online slot machine gaming solutions on a busi-
ness-to-business basis, primarily in Europe and the United States. Evolution operates over 1,700 live tables in its 
studios, run by human dealers. The content is streamed in real-time on its clients’ platforms: traditional land-based 
casinos and pure online gambling sites. Besides proprietary game shows, the firm’s portfolio comprises classics 
such as Live Roulette, Blackjack, and Baccarat. Evolution AB was founded in 2006 and is headquartered in Stock-
holm, Sweden. The company went public in 2015, and its shares are available directly on Nasdaq Stockholm under 
the EVO ticker. On American OTC markets, the ADR uses the ticker symbol EVVTY.

How does the company make money? 
Evolution is responsible for developing and producing online casino content, which can only be played through an 
operator’s interface. The latter provides all essential aspects of the gaming experience, such as the UI, authenti-
cating the players, and managing accounts. Therefore, Evolution is a business-to-business service provider. Its 
clientele includes the majority of the largest online gaming operators and a growing number of land-based casinos 
that have begun to offer games online.
Most of Evolution’s revenues consist of commission fees for live and random-number-generator (RNG) online 
games, calculated as a percentage of the operators’ winnings earned with the supplied content. While official 
communication is unavailable on the exact rates, industry assumptions, and our back-of-the-envelope calculation 
suggest a roughly 10% take rate on a casino’s gross gaming revenue (GGR). For example, if a player loses $10 on a 
bet made on an Evolution-produced game streamed on a Ceasars Casino website, Evolution gets $1 of this sum.
For live gaming content, the most basic agreements include streaming from generic tables, which can be accessed 
by multiple operators simultaneously. They can also request dedicated tables that are reserved and used exclusively 
by the operator and can be entirely customized to their requirements. Evolution charges recurring dedicated table 
fees on these services and a one-time set-up fee for establishing the environment.
It makes sense to disaggregate the top line into two major game categories: live casino and online slot machines. 
The former makes up its core business, contributing 86% of fiscal 2024 sales, while the latter category was added to 
the firm’s portfolio via its 2020 acquisition of NetEnt, one of the leading RNG game providers.

Competitive position and outlook
Regarding online casinos, the live table form has two distinct advantages over RNG-based games. (1) It establish-
es trust toward the operator, which is extremely important as players bet using real money. And (2) it has a social 
element since players can communicate with each other and even develop affection toward the dealer.
Live casino is the bread-and-butter of Evolution, and as management often notes, “The barriers to entry are rel-
atively low, while the barriers to success are considerably higher.” It all comes down to delivering a consistent 
gaming experience for players. A key element lies in establishing trust by eliminating the possibility of fraud and 
minimizing dealer mistakes. This requires specific know-how, tremendous expertise, and sufficient scale, which is 
why land-based casinos and even online operators turn to specialized suppliers such as Evolution. In addition, im-
peccable technology is essential to enable live streaming without glitches, as these could have a serious monetary 
impact on players. Lastly, constant innovation is necessary to broaden the portfolio and make existing games more 
exciting to stay ahead of the competition. We believe Evolution checks all these boxes, dominating both the Europe-
an and U.S. live casino service provider market. By now, it has reached a scale where the virtuous circle of market 
share gains kicks into action. The company has sufficient funds to invest in creating new and alluring games, which 
in turn attract new customers.
Evolution has carved out a royalty-like revenue-sharing structure with casino operators; this also speaks volumes 
about its competitive advantage. Simply put, the business benefits directly from the growing live casino market in-
stead of getting a flat fee. It is interested in providing the best content possible for its customers since their success 
is Evolution’s success, quite literally. 
As more and more countries regulate the online casino segment, licensing and regulatory barriers will render it 
increasingly more difficult for new players to enter the space. Moreover, switching costs help protect Evolution’s 
dedicated studio offerings. Since these tables require a significant upfront investment from the operator, they are 
unlikely to switch to a different supplier after that.
The quantitative side of the story leaves nothing to be desired either, with the company boasting sky-high EVA Mar-
gin and ROC figures. Although the live casino segment is just in its very early innings, Evolution has already demon-
strated the characteristics of a tested, wide-moat business. That said, if the sector were to explode, new competi-
tors could emerge, which needs rigorous monitoring.

Drivers of EVA growth
The overall gambling market (including sports betting, casinos, lottery, and card games) is expected to post low-sin-
gle-digit growth rates, roughly tracking the global GDP. There are two notable trends affecting the industry, though. 
(1) Within the broader gambling market, land-based locations still account for over 75% of the overall pie, while the 
secular shift toward online seems unstoppable. This means that the online gambling segment could grow in the 
double-digit percentage range for years to come. We expect Evolution’s RNG-related revenues to grow along with 
that. 
And (2), within the online casino segment, there is a further secular shift toward live offerings due to the afore-
mentioned reasons of trust and social aspects. As a result, the live casino segment could show the fastest pace 
of growth within the overall market, capable of reaching a CAGR of nearly 20% for the foreseeable future. Over the 
long term, we assume Evolution’s live table revenues will match that since the company should at least be able to 
maintain its dominant market share. However, recent troubles in Asia have led us to take a more conservative top-
line outlook. Yet, as most of its revenues are related to Live offerings, we still expect companywide sales growth to 
hover in the low-double-digit CAGR territory over our forecast period.

Management and capital allocation
Overall, Evolution is led by an extremely competent management team, which is rare to come by. Its two founders 
remained actively engaged with the company, one serving as the chairman of the board and the other as a regular 
board member. They collectively own ~11% of the firm, ensuring that strategic decisions align with the long-term 
interest of shareholders. We also like the absence of a dual-class share structure, unlike how it is with many other 
founder-involved companies. This means that Evolution’s founders hold an equal amount of economic ownership 
and share of voting rights, which is certainly a more favorable corporate governance setting.
Furthermore, the Chief Product Officer (CPO), Todd Haushalter, has been essential to the company’s success. He 
has a deep passion for the industry and oversees the development of new intellectual property (IP) and games, 
which are of major importance for Evolution’s future.
Turning to capital allocation, Evolution is rather a capital-light compounder. Its historical, pre-NetEnt reinvestment 
rates hover around 25%, paired with ROC numbers in nosebleed territory. The primary uses of growth CapEx include 
game development and extending the firm’s global studio footprint. Besides that, Evolution has a 50% dividend 
payout ratio target. The current entry yield is almost 3x the U.S. market average, while growth rates are more than 
decent, and the ability and willingness to continue this streak are clearly there. 
Evolution’s capital allocation framework also paves the way for regular share repurchases or special dividends when 
the company considers the stock’s valuation unfavorable. Taking into account the new €500 million buyback author-
ization for 2025, shareholder distributions are now expected to contribute 6-7 percentage points to the fundamen-
tal return potential.
As far as acquisitions go, even though the firm prefers organic growth, bolt-on deals have been relatively common 
(to broaden Evolution’s offerings). The over $2 billion NetEnt deal in 2020 is certainly an outlier in this regard, and 
the purchase price is increasingly questionable due to prolonged stagnation in the RNG segment lately (although Q3 
2024 results show nice progress). Still, we view the move as strategically sound because it brought the leading RNG 
gaming provider under the Evolution umbrella. This not only enabled the firm to become a one-stop-shop for online 
casino operators but also created the opportunity to lure slot machine players to its live table offerings via a “Go 
live!” option.

What could go wrong?
Even though past evidence suggests otherwise, low barriers to entry could become a problem for Evolution’s 
lucrative live casino offerings, especially in relatively uncharted territories such as the U.S. and Asia. In this rapidly 
growing and extremely profitable industry, new competitors could emerge and undermine the Swedish provider’s 
current revenue-sharing model with a better deal for casino operators. Theoretically, the largest land-based casinos 
could develop the necessary competence and insource the production of online live casino content, posing potential 
threats to suppliers such as Evolution.
Constant innovation is required to stay ahead in this industry, and dependence on key creative personnel might 
become a significant burden over time. Moreover, unlike in video gaming or streaming, where flagship franchises 
such as Call of Duty or Avengers are difficult to copy, we believe that the IP in the world of online casinos is far less 
differentiated. In case a competitor achieves comparable operational excellence, it could slightly tweak Evolution’s 
latest innovations and put a similar game on the market within a short timeframe.
Local regulation of the online gambling industry could also become a limiting factor, even for incumbent players. 
Since roughly 60% of Evolution’s revenue comes from unregulated markets, changes in country-specific laws pose 
a significant threat to the company’s business. While potential regulatory shifts would primarily affect the firm’s 
clients (casino operators), some countries could also force suppliers to obtain licenses. On top of that, local govern-
ments might require the company to operate its live tables physically in the region (which is already the case in the 
few U.S. states that gave the green light to online casinos). This might put pressure on Evolution’s profitability since

Given the ongoing consolidation of the personal luxury goods segment, a potentially value-destroying acquisition

How does the company make money? 
It is worth dissecting the total payment volume (TPV) that flows through PayPal. In fiscal 2024, the company’s core 
branded checkout segment generated 28% of volumes, while its unbranded processing leg (mainly its Braintree 
business) made up about 36% of TPV. During e-commerce transactions, these platforms act as gateways for users 
who can select their credit or debit card or PayPal digital wallet as a payment source. PayPal then charges a fee to 
the merchant (~3%), which is shared with payment processors (e.g., Visa and Mastercard) and financial institutions, 
depending on the funding source. 
The following slice, 25% of companywide TPV, stems from peer-to-peer transactions on Venmo and PayPal, which 
typically do not generate any revenue for the company as these transfers are not monetized. Other merchant ser-
vices, such as volumes associated with invoicing, payouts, and point-of-sale solutions, make up another 9% of TPV, 
while eBay is responsible for the remaining 2% of volumes.
PayPal’s effective monetization rate of overall TPV was about 2% in fiscal 2024. Most of the revenue (90% in fiscal 
2024) is generated through transaction fees charged to merchants and consumers based on the transaction volume 
completed through PayPal’s various platforms. Even though the firm does not report this directly, based on our 
research, the branded checkout business is responsible for the lion’s share of profits derived from transactions, 
while Braintree (for now) is a much less profitable venture. The remaining small fraction (10%) of PayPal’s top line 
stems from so-called “other value-added services” (e.g., subscription packages for merchants enabling checkout 
customization or recurring billing), as well as revenues from interest and fees earned on loans provided through 
PayPal Credit, along with interest earned on underlying customer balances.

Competitive position and outlook
PayPal has amassed considerable scale over the past two decades, reaching 434 million active users and $1.68 
trillion processed annual payment volume in 2024. For comparison, Visa has over 4 billion cards in circulation and 
processes almost $16 trillion in annual payment volume. While PayPal is still a relatively small player in the field, its 
scalable business model is readily apparent in its EVA Margin trajectory (aside from the recent bump in profitabili-

What could go wrong? 
The common root cause behind many of the risks associated with PayPal is the questionable durability of the 
established competitive edge. Although the trifecta of the core checkout business, digital wallets, and payment 
processing provides a unique opportunity, the company has yet to prove that it can form a virtuous cycle reinforcing 
its moat.
While branded checkout remains at the heart of PayPal, the firm is playing defense amid increasing competition 
from the likes of Apple Pay, offering similar functionality (such as the “vault” to store credit card data and one-click 
payment with secure user identification). Despite the digital payment space having a healthy runway for expansion, 
a potential market share loss could seriously hurt PayPal’s growth prospects.
Braintree has established itself as a dominant payment processor, but competition is also fierce in this segment. Its 
biggest peers, such as Stripe or Adyen, have the advantage of solely focusing on the sector, compared to PayPal’s 
divided management attention rooted in its three-legged structure. Also, Braintree is growing at a more rapid rate 
than the firm’s core checkout business. Since it carries structurally lower profitability (coupled with a possible mar-
ket share loss of PayPal’s core checkout button), the growth rate of profits derived from transactions could stay 
under pressure. This metric is crucially important because no matter how far the firm goes in reducing non-transac-
tion-related expenses, cost-cutting is not a sustainable source of incremental EVA growth.
Venmo is still in the early stages of monetization, and while the potential is tremendous, the expected growth tra-
jectory might not materialize if the company fails to extend the platform beyond peer-to-peer transactions. Penetrat-
ing in-store payments could face serious user resistance, as people in Western civilizations are accustomed to the 
frictionless and widely adopted card payment system, which is almost impossible to replicate.
It is now clear that the previous management team (led by Dan Schulman) has done a poor job on multiple fronts. 
The quality of their communication was more than questionable, and so were the rather swift strategic changes,

our reservations about the capital allocation culture he brings from Intuit.

lengthy history of inefficient operations (such as a disproportionately high SG&A overhead) might repeat itself.

How does the company make money? 
Brookfield’s key performance metric is distributable earnings (DE) before realizations, which reflects the recurring

At the core of Brookfield’s earnings is the crown jewel, Brookfield Asset Management (BAM), in which it holds a

The Brookfield ecosystem establishes synergy between its asset manager, insurer, and operating businesses, driv-

At the heart of this virtuous cycle is its BAM alternative asset manager arm, where the connection to the Brook-

are key differentiators. Unlike traditional asset managers (such as BlackRock and T. Rowe Price), which focus more 
heavily on asset classes like equities, fixed income, balanced, and money market funds, alternative asset managers 
such as BAM concentrate on less-liquid investments (including private equity, infrastructure, real estate, and credit 
assets). While the barriers to entry are not particularly significant for the industry, barriers to success are extremely 
high. It takes not only time and skill to build a long enough track record of investment performance to start gather-

expertise and a best-in-class track record in infrastructure, power, renewable energy, real estate, and credit. Accord-

of size that has become a legitimate competitor in this market, following its recent acquisition of Global Infrastruc-
ture Partners (with over $100 billion in AUM). 
The structure of alternative asset managers also differs from traditional peers, as their funds tend to be closed-end 
and very sticky, aligning with the lifecycle of underlying assets. Fee pressure is essentially non-existent, and the 
handful of top players benefit from a healthy organic AUM inflow profile.
From a quantitative standpoint, alternative asset managers operate an extremely lucrative, asset-light business 
model, with BAM boasting 40%+ EVA Margin levels as a standalone entity. However, the picture is far more com-
plex for the parent company, as Brookfield’s outstanding characteristics are not fully captured under the EVA 
framework. The primary reasons behind this are: (1) Financial firms differ from traditional companies in terms of 
invested capital, making the EVA framework insufficient for modeling purposes. For example, Brookfield’s insurance 
arm holds client assets on its balance sheet and earns spread-based income on this capital base. (2) The company 
has a highly complex financial structure, and its stakes in public firms further blur the picture, as the market value 
of these holdings can meaningfully differ from the actual invested capital. (3) As a Canadian firm, Brookfield must 
comply with IFRS standards, which require the consolidation of asset-level debt in its financial statements. This 
creates the impression that Brookfield has more debt outstanding than it actually does.
All in all, Brookfield is a wide-moat, quality compounder worthy of the EVA Monster badge, even though its stellar 
underlying quantitative characteristics are buried within its complex financial structure when viewed through the 
lens of the EVA framework.

opportunities and generate organic inflows. Brookfield stands to benefit from the substantial capital required 
for the green transition of the global energy grid and the buildout of data centers. These initiatives alone demand 
trillions of dollars in incremental investment, with governments and public companies often lacking the necessary 
funds and expertise to develop, manage, and operate these assets. Alternative asset managers like Brookfield are 
well-positioned to bridge this gap.

How does the company make money? 
When dissected by therapeutical areas, diabetes care generated 71% of Novo’s fiscal 2024 sales. Within diabetes, 
GLP-1 treatments are the most significant, contributing 51% of companywide sales. In essence, these drugs mimic 
the action of a hormone called glucagon-like peptide 1. When blood sugar levels start to rise after someone eats, 
these drugs stimulate the body to produce more insulin, which in turn helps lower blood sugar levels. Novo’s major 
GLP-1 diabetes treatments are marketed under the Ozempic and Rybelsus brand names, building on its proprietary 
formula called semaglutide. Besides GLP-1 diabetes treatments, the firm also produces traditional insulin deriva-
tives, responsible for 19% of companywide sales. 
Importantly, GLP-1 therapies have proven effective in treating chronic obesity, mainly through the side effect of help-
ing to curb hunger. Novo markets its obesity treatment drugs under the brand names Wegovy and Saxenda, with 
this rapidly growing therapeutical area already accounting for 23% of the firm’s top line. Lastly, the remaining 6% of 
sales are generated from rare disease care, targeting rare blood and endocrine disorders. As for geographic areas, 
Novo’s revenues are balanced between the U.S. and international markets, with a roughly 60-40 split.

Competitive position and outlook
Novo accounts for one-third of the global diabetes market, holding undisputed leadership positions in both the 
traditional insulin and novel GLP-1 therapeutic areas. Starting with insulin, Novo enjoys a ~45% market share, ben-
efiting from efficient manufacturing techniques and economies of scale. Only two competitors (Sanofi and Eli Lilly) 
can match Novo’s global presence, rendering the insulin market an essential oligopoly between the three players. 
Despite insulin being discovered nearly 100 years ago, the process of making it is more complex than other medica-
tions, leading to little competition and rising consumer prices. Insulin is considered a biological product because it’s 
produced from a living organism (such as yeast and bacteria) and not through chemical synthesis like most other 
manufactured drugs. Biological products can’t be identically duplicated, making it impossible to develop a generic 
insulin option. Instead, manufacturers must produce a biosimilar medication highly similar to the original biologi-
cal compound with no clinically meaningful differences. However, developing and securing approvals for insulin

Drivers of EVA growth
The underlying drivers for growth are firmly in place for Novo, with a vast and underpenetrated total accessible 
market and secular forces supporting a long runway for profitable growth. The global population is aging and 
becoming increasingly overweight, which provides an unfortunate yet powerful tailwind to Novo’s operations in the 
coming decades. On top of that, access to treatment remains meager, with only 15% of the 500+ million people af-
fected by diabetes receiving proper care. The situation is even worse in the area of chronic obesity, where just 2% of 
the estimated 800+ million people get medical treatment for the condition, and recent studies suggest that over 1.5 
billion will be classified as obese by 2035. Unlike diabetes treatments, obesity care is not (yet) covered by most in-
surance policies, so patients bear significant out-of-pocket costs for access (estimated at a few hundred dollars per 
month). However, GLP-1 therapies continue to demonstrate efficacy in treating other conditions that tend to co-oc-
cur with obesity, such as cardiovascular diseases and sleep apnea. Consequently, broad-based insurance coverage 
could be a significant catalyst for making these drugs more accessible to a wider patient base. Additionally, experts 
argue that the overall costs associated with obesity over a patient’s lifetime impose a substantial burden on the 
healthcare system, strengthening the case for introducing insurance coverage for obesity medications. If realized, 
this would represent a significant upside for both Novo and Lilly.
While Novo’s traditional insulin business may stagnate in the foreseeable future, GLP-1 treatments will likely 
continue thriving for years to come. They already account for over 70% of the companywide sales across various 
indications. Strong double-digit sales growth is anticipated for Novo’s diabetes and obesity GLP-1 therapies, which 
could propel companywide growth rates to the low-double-digit territory over our 5-year forecast period. As for 
profitability, we assume a broadly stable EVA Margin in the 30-35% range for the next several years. Although 
increasing sales might bring about some leverage over SG&A expenses, we expect the rising levels of R&D spending 
to offset any incremental economies of scale effect.

Over the past decade, more than $2 trillion has been withdrawn from actively managed mutual funds, as most can-

What could go wrong? 
In our view, the most prominent risk for Diageo is that distilled spirits, particularly the premium segment and 
above, tend to be more cyclical than other beverage categories, like beer. These products are responsible for ~80% 
of the company’s top line. Heightened macroeconomic volatility and increased inflation (subsequently, the cost of 
living across many countries) could adversely affect consumer spending power. Morningstar estimates that the 
correlation between GDP per capita growth and the spirits industry retail value has been 0.8 over the last decade, but 
only 0.3 for retail beer sales.
On another note, health and wellness trends suggest that, over time, fewer people may opt to drink alcoholic bev-
erages. Those who do are likely to moderate their consumption due to the well-documented health risks of excessive 
drinking. Furthermore, early studies suggest that novel weight-loss drugs like semaglutide and other GLP-1 agonists 
may effectively treat alcohol misuse and reduce overall consumption in treated patients. While Diageo benefits from 
the “less but better” premiumization trend in the alcohol market, widespread adoption of these drugs among its 
middle-class and affluent customers could negatively impact long-term volumes, potentially limiting Diageo’s sales 
growth.
Ineffective brand protection and failure to address counterfeiting of Diageo’s products could damage the firm’s 
brand reputation and harm consumers. Any combination of these factors may result in an inability to exert signif-
icant pricing power, damaging Diageo’s growth prospects since regular price increases are one of its key revenue 
drivers. 
Regulatory intervention is also something to consider. Public health concerns may prompt regulators to ban or 
restrict the marketing of alcoholic drinks, raise excise taxes, or substantially limit retail alcohol availability (as seen 
in Russia). Increased trade tensions between nations could also lead to additional barriers, hindering further interna-
tional expansion.
In terms of capital allocation, regular M&A is a part of Diageo’s DNA. While the previous management team had 
a decent track record, the arrival of a new leader always introduces some level of uncertainty. We’d prefer that they 
keep megadeals off the table. Since a large portion of the company’s costs incur in British pounds, there is also a 
foreign currency risk. Lastly, firms in the alcohol space are often considered members of the “sin stock” group, 
which could potentially translate to unjustified and permanently lower valuation multiples from the market.

What could go wrong? 
Nike sells premium footwear and apparel priced above “no-name” alternatives. Even though its core customers 
belong to the global middle class (making them less price-sensitive and more resilient), pressures on discretionary 
budgets could lead to downtrading to lower-priced brands or outright postponing purchases. The good news, how-
ever, is that unlike cars or kitchen appliances, replacing worn-out shoes cannot be delayed for years, making Nike 
more resilient against macroeconomic cycles compared to those selling consumer durable goods.
Despite the smooth progression toward direct sales, Nike still heavily relies on third-party department stores for 
its sales. Not only are physical retailers struggling to stay afloat under pressure from e-commerce players, but 
these outlets also play a significant role in positioning the brand. Excessive price cuts or promotion of other “hotter” 
brands is at their discretion, which could indirectly harm Nike’s pricing power on its direct sales platforms and dam-
age brand perception.
Given the relatively low barriers to entry, competition is cutthroat in this space. Nike’s footwear business must 
stand its ground against high-performance shoemakers such as Hoka or Brooks, while its apparel arm faces lulu-
lemon as one of its fiercest competitors, alongside several white-label brands blooming on platforms like Shein or 
Temu. Even the sneaker category is saturated, competing with luxury brands such as Gucci or Dior and a plethora of 
premium alternatives, including Lacoste or Ralph Lauren. As a countermeasure, Nike needs to defend its brand repu-
tation through consistent, industry-leading spending on innovation and marketing. Should the company fail to do this 
due to short-termism, its market position could inevitably suffer.
Lastly, Nike has grown increasingly dependent on Chinese customers. Over 15% of its top line stems from the 
region, producing growth rates that surpass those of the company as a whole. The local sportswear market is highly 
competitive, and nationalism has boosted native brands such as Anta. Whether Nike can preserve its leading market 
share in the country for decades to come remains an open question.

Glossary

Adjusted Funds From Operations (AFFO): This calculation takes a REIT’s FFO (see later) and subtracts any recur-
ring expenditure that is capitalized and then amortized, as well as any straight-lining of rents.

Capital: Also known as “net business assets.” It is all assets used in business operations, net of trade funding from 
accounts payable and accrued expenses. (It is also equal to the total amount of debt and equity raised from inves-
tors or retained from earnings.) Numerous adjustments are made to remedy accounting distortions. For instance, 
capital is measured net of excess cash, net of deferred tax assets, and net of pension and retirement assets, but 
including leased assets, and after capitalizing and amortizing research and development (R&D) and advertising 
spending over time.

CEO: Chief Executive Officer

CFO: Chief Financial Officer

Compound Annual Growth Rate (CAGR): It is the annualized average rate of growth between two given years, as-
suming growth takes place at an exponentially compounded rate.

CROIC: Cash Return On Invested Capital. This indicator shows how much free cash flow per dollar the business gen-
erates from invested capital. This can be the ultimate performance metric as it clearly shows how effective manage-
ment is and the strength of the business. (CROIC = FCF/Invested Capital)

Dividend yield: The percentage of the price per share a company pays out to its shareholders as dividends annually, 
calculated by dividing the forecasted 12-month dividend payout by the current price.

Dividend Growth Rate (DGR): The compound annual growth rate of the dividend for the periods shown.

EBITDA: Earnings before interest, taxes, depreciation, and amortization is essentially net income with these things 
added back to it. It is used to analyze and compare profitability without the effects of finance and accounting deci-
sions.

EBITDAR: EBITDA plus add-backs that include (1) rent expense, (2) R&D and advertising spending, (3) reported 
retirement expense less the service cost, and (4) the changes in bookkeeping reserves for bad debts, LIFO, warranty 
expense, etc. EBITDAR is an improved version of EBITDA that is a better, purer, more comparable measure of true 
cash operating profit.

Enterprise Value (EV): See Market Value (MV) below.

Economic Value Added (EVA): It measures the company’s economic profit after deducting all costs, including the 
cost of giving the stock’s investors a full, fair, and competitive return on their investment in the business. EVA is a 
trademark of Stern Stewart & Co. The data is provided by Institutional Shareholder Services Inc.

EVA Margin: EVA/Sales. It is the percentage of sales that ends up as EVA after all operating expenses, taxes, and 
capital charges have been paid. This indicator consolidates pricing power, operational efficiency, and the quality of 
asset management into one overall score that effectively measures business model productivity.

This is because real estate historically rises and falls with macroeconomic conditions, and any operating results 
calculated using the cost accounting method are not an accurate measure of performance.

Market Value (MV): The value of the company’s debt and equity capital, given its share price, net of excess cash, 
and assuming that the book value of liabilities approximates their market value. The same as Enterprise Value (EV).

Net Operating Profit After Taxes (NOPAT): It is the operating profit before any interest or financing charges are 
deducted, measured net of period charges for depreciation and amortization, and after taxes computed using a 
smoothed normal tax provision, net of the cost of capital saved from deferring taxes, and after corrective adjust-
ments to remedy accounting distortions. NOPAT measures the free cash flow from operations that is distributable 
after ensuring that tangible and intangible assets needed to sustain the profit can be replenished.

NOPAT yield: NOPAT divided by market value (MV).

Operating Cash Flow (OCF): The measure of cash into or out of the company, similar to Net Income but less easily 
manipulated and without the effects of depreciation and other non-cash charges.

Payout ratio: Dividend payout ratio is Dividend Per Share as a percent of Diluted Earnings Per Share and can be 
used to measure the chance of a dividend increase or cut. For example, a company with a small payout ratio has 
room to increase its dividend.

Return on Capital (ROC): NOPAT divided by average capital. It is the after-tax rate of return on net business assets.

Sales: Net sales are operating revenues earned by a company for selling its products or rendering its services. It is 
also called net revenue or the top line.

TTM: Trailing twelve months. (The same as TFQ, trailing four quarters.)

If you are saving and investing regularly, it is a wise strategy to buy the stock(s) from the Top Picks section (on

purchased at attractive prices.

If you have a lump sum to invest, getting that money to work inevitably becomes an exercise in regret minimization. 
Ask yourself what you’d regret more: missing out on further gains in the market (by waiting on the sidelines) or 
taking part in large losses (after investing a substantial one-off payment without delay). Studies unequivocally show 
that you would be better off investing all the money at once. Should you divide that lump sum into X equal parts and 
invest it gradually over the next X months, the longer you wait to deploy the cash, the bigger your likely underper-
formance will be compared to the “invest at once” approach. However, this (most likely underperforming) dollar-cost 
averaging method may have two advantages. (1) The psychological: it helps avoid feelings of regret if the market 
takes a downturn after you invest. (2) By giving yourself more time to deploy your money, Mr. Market may present 
more opportunities, so you may end up with a more diversified, better-balanced portfolio. At the same time, numer-
ous research indicates that it’s prudent to invest a lump sum immediately. Weigh your emotionally based concerns 
carefully against the facts. Delaying investment is a form of market timing, which I consider futile. With anything 
less than 2-3 years of my regular savings, I’d go with lump-sum investing without a second thought. I’d most likely 
also pick that route with an inheritance or any other windfall. Please see the opening piece of the March 2023 issue 
for all the details.

You may also have your own criteria, like “the dividend yield must be above 3%.” In this case, pick the stocks from 
the Top Picks that fulfill your requirements, and do so every month. That said, if you notice that none of today’s best 
opportunities can jump over your bar, it may be time to think about whether your expectations are still realistic in the 
prevailing market environment.

reap the rewards of diversification and have a portfolio of top companies from various sectors. At the same time,
