Is More Always Better?

“We really can tell you in five minutes whether we’re interested in 
something.” (Warren Buffett at Berkshire’s 1997 annual meeting)

class psychologist and a peer of Nobel laureate Daniel 
Kahneman that gives us the answers. Slovic asked eight 
seasoned professional handicappers who made their liv-
ings solely on their gambling skills to predict the winner 
of 40 horse races. In round one, the predictions had to 
be made based on the 5 pieces of information the par-
ticipants opted to receive. (They were allowed to rank 
the available information by perceived importance, from 
a list of 88 variables.) With 5 pieces of info, the handi-
cappers were 17 percent accurate (that is much better 
than the 10 percent chance of a blind guess with 10 
horses in each race). Their confidence level was 19 per-
cent, almost exactly matching their accuracy. So far so 
good, but fast forward to the fourth round, where they 
were given 40 pieces of information: accuracy flatlined 
at 17 percent, while confidence nearly doubled to 34 
percent. All in all, the results obtained were consistent 
with the aforementioned football study – accuracy was 
consistent despite more information becoming available, 
but confidence increased as the number of available 
statistics rose.

As investors, we now have easy access to more infor-
mation than ever to guide our decision making. Apart 
from the challenge of disentangling meaningful news 
from a blizzard of noise, information overload has other 
serious consequences that are worth examining. Most 
people believe that more information can improve the 
accuracy of decision making, thus making us more con-
fident about our judgment. There have been numerous 
studies in this area, however, and the conclusions speak 
for themselves.

In 2008, Tsai, Klayman, and Hastie tested the impact of 
additional information on an individual’s ability to predict 
the results of college football games and their confi-
dence in doing so correctly. Participants had to forecast 
a winner for a number of games based on anonymized 
statistical information that came in blocks of 6. For the 
first round of predictions the participants had 6 pieces 
of data, and after each round of predictions, they were 
given another block of information, up to 5 blocks (or 30 
data points), and had to update their views. Participants 
were asked to predict both the winner of the match and 
their confidence in their judgment between 50% and 
100%. The aim of the experiment was to understand 
how increased information impacted both accuracy 
and confidence. It is important to note that the blocks 
of information were provided at random, and the par-
ticipants were amateurs. The results are eye-opening: 
decisions were little better with 30 statistics than just 6. 
However, participants’ confidence that they could select 
the winner increased materially and consistently. When 
getting more seemingly relevant info, our confidence lev-
el skyrockets, although it is not justified by the accuracy 
of our decisions.

Before we move on, I want to emphasize that I am not 
equating investing to gambling but still consider the 
conclusions of these studies important for us. (In fact, 
long-term investing is really the opposite of gambling. 
The longer you gamble, the greater the odds that you 
lose because the house has the edge. The longer you 
invest, the greater the odds that you generate positive 
returns provided that you follow a sound strategy.) 
Results show that adding more information makes the 
confidence and accuracy levels diverge, and being more 
confident means that we may be eager to increase the 
size of our bets although the stagnating level of accu-
racy doesn’t justify this.

information were provided at random to make matters 
worse. Don’t you think the same conclusion would hold 
for professionals who were able to select the informa-

Pearson was a gambling legend. Born dirt poor and with

The FALCON Method involves a handful of factors that 
are all historically proven to increase the probability of 
good performance. Could we use more data? Of course, 
but we would hardly benefit from the additional inputs 
while we could fall prey to the side-effects mentioned 
above. You may also notice that more information 
tends to feed our confirmation bias (that is the tenden-
cy to search for, interpret, favor, and recall information 
in a way that confirms our preexisting beliefs), and 
makes us more confident while we are quick to ignore 
or dismiss information that conflicts with our original 
assessment. Human psychology is part and parcel of 
investing as I often point out. Accumulating and pro-
cessing more information also entails the danger of 
becoming attached to our ideas. After all, “I have put 
in hundreds of hours to research this company, so it 
is only natural that I am married to my position at this 
point”. With less information involved, there is not 
much sacrifice on the accuracy side, yet we remain 
more open to reevaluating our positions and admitting 
our mistakes. (I am ready to concede that I consider 
Tanger Outlets a far more risky bet today than when I 
included it in our FALCON Portfolio. It is only a sleep-
well-at-night type of stock for narcoleptic investors and 
analysts who do not miss a single day to publish their 
opinion on every available platform. At current levels, 
risks may be priced in, but denying their existence is 
simply unprofessional.)

Those three things are vital for investing as well. We 
must make our stock purchases when the odds are 
skewed in our favor. Even then, we must size our posi-
tions sensibly. Last but not least, we must never forget 
that human psychology is part of the equation. As a 
closing, let me quote Peter Bernstein, who once said, 
“The fundamental law of investing is the uncertainty of 
the future.” As investors, our challenge is to translate 
those uncertainties into probabilities and payoffs in the 
search for attractive securities.

You must understand that even a horse with a very 
high likelihood of winning can be either a very good or a 
very bad bet and that the difference between the two is 
determined by only one thing: the odds. The same goes 
for stock investing; neither a good (i.e., high-return) 
business nor a bad (low-return) business is inherently 
attractive or unattractive. Investors need to assess the 
stocks of all companies versus expectations that are 
baked into the market prices. That said, even the stock 
of a bad company can offer a good investment oppor-
tunity if it is largely mispriced, but I still stick with the 
top quality businesses and wait for decent odds (good 
enough prices) to include them in my portfolio. That’s 
my game, since buying bad stocks would keep me up at 
night, regardless of the valuation I paid. With the FAL-
CON Method, we are selective contrarians who enjoy 
the process as much as the proceeds, and this attitude 
keeps us going when times are tough. Now let’s see 
what the FALCON spots this time!

All in all, there are probably less relevant pieces of infor-
mation out there than most people think. Knowing what 
really matters is key to successful long-term investing, 
but it still doesn’t decrease the importance of our last 
line of defense that is diversification. Michael Maubous-

Opinion

Home Depot is the world’s largest home improvement retailer, eclipsing $108 billion in revenue in 2018. The 
company’s economies of scale is the main source of its moat that results in consistent shareholder value creation. 
Recent results show good demand across both the pro and do-it-yourself customer and the growth of the digital 
business even at a time when headwinds are increasing across the home improvement landscape (with rising 
interest rates and declining existing home sales). The acquisition of Interline Brands in 2015 allowed Home Depot 
to enter the maintenance, repair, and operations sector and this is the exact business that can provide sticky, 
consistent customers driving incremental purchases even with a housing slowdown. Home Depot is perceived 
to be the better run company compared to Lowe’s (the latter intends to change this with its recent management 
shakeup), and thus its shares usually trade at a premium valuation. As to the valuation, this is the part of the story 
that I still cannot fall in love with at the moment. Although the operation and capital allocation dimensions look 
good, I don’t feel that the valuation is compelling enough to initiate a position in HD.

Opinion

There are not too many dividend growth stories that are more spectacular than TXN’s: the company’s EPS has 
grown nearly 450% since it began to steadily increase its dividend in 2004 and the dividend has gone up by a com-
pound annual rate of almost 26% in the same period.  Concerns about the trade war with China have hurt the stock 
price since shipments of products into China typically represent a large portion of the firm’s revenue. Texas Instru-
ments has just recently announced a dividend increase of 24.2%, and it is also extraordinary that the company has 
essentially cut its share count in half in less than 15 years. Such a repurchase activity really helps the feasibility of 
large dividend increases.
Texas Instruments has a leading share of the fragmented yet lucrative analog chip market. Switching costs in this 
segment provide somewhat of a moat to TXN since it is difficult to swap out analog chips for competing offerings 
once they are designed into a specific electronic device. What makes this business even more attractive is that 
analog chips tend to make up only a small portion of a product’s bill of materials thus purchasing decisions are 
based on performance rather than price, helping Texas Instruments retain pricing power as long as it can maintain 
the superior quality. The company’s size allows it to compete across a broader spectrum of industries, without its

Glossary

Adjusted funds from operations (AFFO): This calculation takes a REIT’s FFO (see later) and subtracts any recurring 
expenditure that is capitalized and then amortized, as well as any straight-lining of rents.

Blended P/E: A price-to-earnings indicator, where the earnings part is the calculated as the weighted average of the 
most recent actual reported earnings plus the closest quarterly forecast earnings. This gives the most weight to the 
past “actual” reported earnings, but also includes an appropriate consideration of the company’s continuing earnings 
power post their last report.

CEO: Chief Executive Officer

CFO: Chief Financial Officer

CROIC: Cash Return On Invested Capital. This indicator shows how much free cash flow per dollar the business 
generates from invested capital. This can be the ultimate performance metric as it shows so clearly how effective 
management is and the strength of the business. (CROIC = FCF/Invested Capital)

Dividend yield: The percentage of the price per share a company pays out to its shareholders as dividends annually, 
calculated by dividing the forecasted 12-month dividend payout by the current price.

DGR: or Dividend Growth Rate is the compound annual growth rate of the dividend for the periods shown.

EBITDA: Earnings before interest, taxes, depreciation, and amortization is essentially net income with these things 
added back to it. It is used to analyze and compare profitability without the effects of finance and accounting deci-
sions.

EVP: Executive Vice President

Ex-Dividend Date: If you purchase a stock on its ex-dividend date or after, you will not receive the next dividend pay-
ment. Instead, the seller gets the dividend. If you purchase before the ex-dividend date, you get the dividend.

FCF payout ratio: This alternative dividend payout ratio aims to be more accurate by excluding accounting earnings 
and including only actual cash generation. It is calculated as Dividend Per Share as a percent of Free Cash Flow per 
Share.

FCF yield: Free Cash Flow per Share for the trailing 12-months divided by the current stock price. This is the inverse 
of the Price to Free Cash Flow (PFCF) value.

Free cash flow (FCF): Free Cash Flow shows how much cash a company generates after paying to maintain and 
expand its production. It is calculated by subtracting Capital Expenditure (Cap Ex) from Cash Flow.

Funds from operations (FFO): This refers to the figure used by real estate investment trusts (REITs) to define the 
cash flow from their operations. It is calculated by adding depreciation and amortization to earnings, subtracting any 
gains on sales. Many leading REITs believe that the FFO calculation is the best presentation of operating results. 
This is because real estate historically rises and falls with macroeconomic conditions, and any operating results 
calculated using the cost accounting method are not an accurate measure of performance.

Payout Ratio: Dividend payout ratio is Dividend Per Share as a percent of Diluted Earnings Per Share and can be

ny is profiting more from issuing new stock than it is spending on buybacks and dividends.
