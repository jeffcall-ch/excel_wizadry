Revisiting Our Tanger Investment

“Winning and losing are only loose signals of decision quality. 
You can win lucky hands and lose unlucky ones.” (Annie Duke, 
professional poker player)

It was never a question of “if” but “when”, so I was not 
too shocked to see the first negative dividend announce-
ment hit our FALCON Portfolio when Tanger Outlets 
(SKT) suspended its dividend until further notice. You 
received an email alert with my assessment of the situ-
ation on May 12, yet our Tanger position does deserve 
further inspection. Judging by the numbers, my decision 
to include this stock in the FALCON Portfolio looks a 
clear mistake. Of course, hindsight is always 20/20, but 
I’m not the one to let such a learning opportunity go to 
waste, so let’s revisit what happened!

As the months went by, I highlighted in November 2017 
that the short interest was above 20% of the float. Since 
short-sellers have a limited profit and potentially unlimit-
ed losses, they tend to perform thorough due diligence. 
So in such cases, you must always ask the question: 
What’s their thesis? If you can’t disprove their bearish 
viewpoints, then don’t buy the stock. This red flag got 
me to emphasize that “no sure bet exists in investing, 
and while Tanger deserves a place in our portfolio, it 
makes no sense to increase the size of this position to 
irrational levels”. Nevertheless, I was still comfortable 
buying the stock.

Tanger is a simple, easy-to-understand business: the 
company owns and operates outlet centers. This was 
one of the first stocks in our FALCON Portfolio, and 
after re-reading all the previous newsletters and the info 
available back in July 2017, I still think that our original 
buy decision was warranted. We had a 5.3% entry yield 
with very safe payout ratios and an immaculate divi-
dend history from a self-funding REIT led by a proven 
management team. Since the numbers looked fine, the 
real work could begin. As Charlie Munger says, when 
you locate a bargain, you must ask, “Why me, God? Why 
am I the only one who could find this bargain?” If you 
don’t know why the company is cheap, you don’t know 
the situation well enough yet, so you shouldn’t buy the 
stock. I highlighted the main problem in my very first 
Tanger opinion piece: “Sales of the underlying properties 
have been stagnant for 5 years, meaning that sales have 
been falling inflation-adjusted. This is beginning to show 
on the tenant occupancy cost (rent as a percentage 
of gross sales), which has climbed from 7.4% to 9.9% 
between 2006 and 2016.” Unless sales performance 
improves, tenants are not too happy about the continu-
ous rent increases, as occupancy costs are nearing that 
of the better-quality malls. Back in 2017, I thought that 
this was just another cyclical downturn for Tanger, and 
its management had navigated through several of these. 
I foresaw no tectonic shift in the retail landscape.

I voiced my concern about the CEO turning evasive on 
buyback-related questions in December 2017, but this 
concern was more than offset by the appearance of 
activist investors, hedge funds, and strategic investors 
in the retail REIT segment as I considered this latter a 
clear sign of the divergence between stock prices and 
underlying business values. I was cautiously optimistic, 
and the stock price started 2018 at the level where we 
bought in, so the first few months were a non-event.

2018 brought a series of guidance cuts, and the Tanger 
story changed dramatically in May when the company 
disclosed its extensive use of short-term leases to keep 
the stores occupied. As I pointed out back then, “some 
calculations suggest that short-term leases could only 
be done 25% cheaper than previously. This huge nega-
tive leasing spread is much more concerning than the 
slightly lowered guidance!” Management has dubbed 
this “standard practice”, and I had a feeling that they 
switched to a sugarcoating style of communication. Any 
analyst who turned a blind eye to these events is hard 
to take seriously, in my opinion. (More on this later.) I 
noted in the May 2018 newsletter that “I already have a 
large enough position in SKT shares, so I need to stay 
disciplined and stop adding to it regardless of the 
stock price.” The stock traded at $20.77.

As to the valuation, in April 2019, I wrote that “If today’s 
‘flat-to-modestly declining Tanger’ is the new normal, 
then I wouldn’t call the shares significantly mispriced.” I 
emphasized in the Closing Thoughts of that same issue 
that the fundamentals of the company have deteriorated 
significantly since we included it in our FALCON Portfo-

a high-yielding position. All in all, 2019 brought a contin-
uous slide of Tanger’s stock price, and I started to feel 
that this may be a classic value trap situation. A value 
trap is a company that looks cheap based on prior 
results but is expensive based on future earnings. As 
the price sunk deeper and deeper, quarterly data always 
came to back up the lower price, but at those lower 
levels, the stock did look cheap! (As the saying goes, 
“Everything looks cheap as it is going to zero.”)

I’m not a fan of naming and shaming, yet I think there is 
something more to learn here. Brad Thomas is “Seeking 
Alpha’s number one REIT analyst” with 86,400 follow-
ers. For my taste, he was not too conservative when he 
called Tanger a bargain with an entry yield of 3.3% in 
May 2015. As years went by, he labeled the stock “the 
strongest buy”, “blue chip on sale”, and “textbook strong 
buy” before he finally “decided to officially downgrade 
Tanger to a Sell” on April 1, 2020, at a price of $5.05. 
(Verba volant, scripta manent. You can check out his re-
lated articles here.) I believe that we were more cautious 
with our entry, and we didn’t turn a blind eye when the 
situation changed because we don’t win bets by being 
in love with our ideas. We win bets by relentlessly striv-
ing to calibrate our beliefs and predictions to represent 
the world more accurately. That said, I don’t think that 
throwing in the towel is the wisest decision at this point, 
so I’m not selling my Tanger shares.

Tanger hasn’t appeared among my top picks since May 
2019, and I dubbed it “a rather speculative, high-risk 
bet” in the July 2019 issue stating that the stock was 
a hold at $16.34 until we see some improvement in 
the key metrics. After management was not willing to 
disclose the success rate at which they could convert 
the short-term pop-up stores to longer-term permanent 
stores, I was not too impressed by the insider purchases 
at ~$14.5 in August 2019. At that point, I was well in the 
“patiently waiting phase” with my Tanger investment. 
The coronavirus was an unexpected, huge external 
shock that acted as the nail in the coffin. No investor 
could foresee this coming. That said, Tanger’s manage-
ment already gave signs of the lack of candor in 2017 
when the CEO was too tired to answer very relevant 
questions on occupancy, FFO per share guidance, and 
leasing spreads. (See the transcript of the Q3 2017 earn-
ings call.) Fast forward to today when management is 
unwilling to disclose the percentage of tenants that have 
accepted the rent deferral terms offered in late March. 
Basically, 1.5 months after they offered those terms, 
they were still only assuming that those tenants that 
didn’t pay would accept. If they won’t Tanger may be in 
a very strong position contractually, but its business 
model may be broken.

Mr. Market may have been right with this one, but he 
was certainly wrong when he handed us Target at $52. 
We won’t win all our bets. I still don’t think I should 
have sold Tanger on the first negative signs. That kind 
of mentality would make me sell almost all my selective 
contrarian bets prematurely since they don’t become 
Mr. Market’s favorite right after my purchase. This 
month’s recommended read is Thinking in Bets, a guide 
to selecting and applying decision strategies in uncer-
tainty. And now that we are back to our good old pro-
cess-outcome matrix, I really feel that life is simple, it’s 
only us who make it complicated. To some degree, we’re 
all outcome junkies, but the more we wean ourselves 
from that addiction, the happier we’ll be. Focus on the 
process, and the results will take care of themselves. At 
the end of the day, I still don’t know whether outlets will 
survive. Now let’s see what the FALCON spots this time!

My take on the dividend profile:
The latest management comments suggest that the dividend payout ratio target (of approximately 80% of adjusted 
diluted EPS) may be shelved for 2020, and Altria may lean on its balance sheet and strong cash flow to support 
the dividend. The Board always has the final say, but it is hard to foresee a dividend cut at this point. Altria has 
increased its dividend 54 times in 50 years, and this pretty much sums up what a reliable income machine this 
stock can be. Tobacco companies usually operate with high payout ratios, so these metrics alone are no reason to 
worry. The entry yield is hard to resist, but the buyback authorization has been revoked, and dividend growth may be 
minuscule as Altria needs to give itself some financial breathing room after the Juul and Cronos deals. (MO stock 
provided a higher than 8.0% yield in 6.2% of the time within the last 5 years.)

Opinion
The company is arguably one of the best performers in stock market history and, as such, has become somewhat of 
a religion stock. However, as past performance is no indication of what may happen in the future, investors should 
focus on the headwinds Altria is facing today. U.S. cigarette shipment volumes are declining, but Altria has been 
able to combat the trend with continuous price increases and market share gains. However, this share growth has 
effectively leveled off over the past 7-8 years. 
The company is a money-printing machine (just like Philip Morris); they not only convert a really high amount of 
revenues into cash flows, but the business itself requires minimal investment to maintain itself. It is no surprise 
that most of the free cash flow is returned to shareholders through dividends and share repurchases. The company 
made proactive strategic investments in rapidly growing categories (vaping and cannabis) that management 
believes would strengthen their long-term financial profile and enhance their growth prospects.

The most recent quarter: As to the first-quarter report, adjusted diluted EPS grew by 18.5%, driven by an excellent 
performance from the core tobacco businesses. (That said, the other parts of Altria are a complete mess.) After a 
short pause, all of the company’s manufacturing facilities are operating. The smokeable products segment delivered 
solid first-quarter results with higher pricing and higher volumes. Volume figures are a little tricky to analyze, though. 
As noted on the call, “When adjusted for the traditional factors of trade inventories calendar differences and other 
factors, our domestic cigarette volume decreased by 3.5%.” Also, “preliminary estimates of consumer pantry loading 
should be an adjusting factor to reported volumes due to its high likelihood of near-term volume payback. When 
adjusted for these traditional factors and the estimated consumer pantry loading, our domestic cigarette volume 
decreased by 5%.” In fact, the early data in April does seem to indicate payback of some of that pantry loading. 
Management anticipates a recessionary backdrop and increased financial pressure on adult tobacco consumers, 
and they think that elevated unemployment rates will persist throughout the year, causing an increase in down-
trading.
In the first quarter, total e-vapor estimated volumes declined by 12%, sequentially, and 10% versus the prior year. 
Management attributes the volume decline partially to January’s FDA guidance, which bans all nontobacco or 
menthol-flavored pod products. As to the IQOS commercialization, the COVID-19 outbreak has disrupted that 
segment big time as person-to-person contact was an important part of Altria’s strategy.
It is remarkable that Altria maintained its full-year 2020 adjusted industry decline rate estimate of 4% to 6%. For me, 
this shows that tobacco is resilient, especially the cigarette category. On the other hand, Altria’s alcohol assets are 
performing rather poorly. For me, it wouldn’t be surprising to see Altria dispose of its AB InBev stake in 2021 when 
the lockup period expires.
As to the financial strength, the newly appointed CEO had this to say on the call: “We’re fortunate that our 
businesses are highly cash generative and convert income to cash at over 90%. However, we believe it was prudent 
to take additional actions to preserve financial flexibility in this environment. In the first quarter, we halted share 
repurchases and fully drew down our $3 billion revolving credit facility as a precautionary measure. This week, the 
Board rescinded our existing share repurchase program, which had a $500 million balance to further strengthen 
our liquidity position.” While buybacks are stopped, the commitment to the dividend seems intact, although I 
would prefer a much more straightforward communication on this matter from a Dividend Champion. For me, 
management’s most recent comments mean that the dividend payout may exceed the long-term target (that is 80% 
of adjusted diluted EPS) this year as Altria’s reported EPS may be impacted by AB InBev’s poor performance. This 
would make sense as ABI had already cut its dividend, so it is not that significant of a contributor to Altria’s cash 
flow generation but has a more considerable impact on the EPS line.
What’s unchanged: Consumers are still interested in satisfying nicotine products, so the nicotine business is 
not dead, but the predictability and the profit growth that was once provided largely by combustible cigarettes is 
temporarily (?) gone. Dividend increases are unlikely to exceed mid-single-digit levels in the coming years. Altria 
trades at an attractive valuation as long as we are ready to bet on the company’s management to successfully 
diversify the firm’s income stream without destroying more value. As I said before, an entry yield of ‘7% or better’ 
is where I would load up on Altria if I hadn’t already owned a decently sized position in the stock. The annualized 
total return could reach double-digits, even in the most conservative scenarios. Please note that while Altria may be 
a reasonable long-term income investment as part of a well-diversified portfolio, you must size this stock position 
properly and not get overexcited about the high entry yield! No meaningful updates since last month’s newsletter. 
(Disclosure: I own MO stock in my portfolio.)

Opinion

I tend to avoid investing in banks in general, as these companies are almost impossible to analyze. Even Warren 
Buffett admits this in his letters to shareholders. I am making an exception for M&T Bank, though, since this midsize 
bank is managed so excellently that even Berkshire Hathaway decided to take a stake in it. 
When it comes to banks, poor underwriting tends to be the largest risk to shareholders’ capital, and M&T has ex-
celled in this regard. This bank underwrites loans better than its peers, as evidenced by its low charge-offs during 
the financial crisis, which allowed M&T to maintain cash payouts to shareholders during some of the toughest years 
for banks. Operating efficiency is excellent, and I also like the savvy acquisitions through which M&T purchased 
distressed banks at reasonable prices. Thus the company could gain access to worthwhile regions and wealthy 
customers and has been effective in integrating operations.
M&T does face near-term challenges, but these seem to be manageable over the long term. The company derives 
about two-thirds of its income from interest income on loans. Much of its loan book is composed of commercial 
loans that tend to be the fastest to reprice in a falling interest-rate environment. The remaining one-third of revenue 
comes from non-banking businesses like wealth management or deposit service fees, which are less sensitive to 
interest rates.
On the negative side, M&T’s profitability is largely determined by the interest rate cycle and the effects of credit and 
debt cycles, none of which is under management’s control. From peak to trough in 2007-2009, the S&P 500 returned 
-55% while M&T performed even worse, as its stock returned a loss of -68% during the Great Recession. Despite the 
company’s ability to maintain its dividend during this time, M&T may not be the best option for risk-averse inves-
tors who lose sight of the fundamentals and tend to panic in such situations.

Glossary

Adjusted funds from operations (AFFO): This calculation takes a REIT’s FFO (see later) and subtracts any recurring 
expenditure that is capitalized and then amortized, as well as any straight-lining of rents.

Blended P/E: A price-to-earnings indicator, where the earnings part is the calculated as the weighted average of the 
most recent actual reported earnings plus the closest quarterly forecast earnings. This gives the most weight to the 
past “actual” reported earnings, but also includes an appropriate consideration of the company’s continuing earnings 
power post their last report.

CEO: Chief Executive Officer

CFO: Chief Financial Officer

CROIC: Cash Return On Invested Capital. This indicator shows how much free cash flow per dollar the business 
generates from invested capital. This can be the ultimate performance metric as it shows so clearly how effective 
management is and the strength of the business. (CROIC = FCF/Invested Capital)

Dividend yield: The percentage of the price per share a company pays out to its shareholders as dividends annually, 
calculated by dividing the forecasted 12-month dividend payout by the current price.

DGR: or Dividend Growth Rate is the compound annual growth rate of the dividend for the periods shown.

EBITDA: Earnings before interest, taxes, depreciation, and amortization is essentially net income with these things 
added back to it. It is used to analyze and compare profitability without the effects of finance and accounting deci-
sions.

EVA (Economic Value Added): It measures the stock’s economic profit – the profit remaining after deducting all 
costs, including the cost of giving the stock’s investors a full, fair, and competitive return on their investment in the 
business.

EVA Margin: EVA/Sales. It is the percentage of sales that ends up as EVA after all operating expenses, taxes, and 
capital charges have been paid. This indicator consolidates pricing power, operational efficiency and the quality of 
asset management into one overall score.

EVP: Executive Vice President

Ex-Dividend Date: If you purchase a stock on its ex-dividend date or after, you will not receive the next dividend pay-
ment. Instead, the seller gets the dividend. If you purchase before the ex-dividend date, you get the dividend.

FCF payout ratio: This alternative dividend payout ratio aims to be more accurate by excluding accounting earnings 
and including only actual cash generation. It is calculated as Dividend Per Share as a percent of Free Cash Flow per 
Share.

FCF yield: Free Cash Flow per Share for the trailing 12-months divided by the current stock price. This is the inverse 
of the Price to Free Cash Flow (PFCF) value.

Free cash flow (FCF): Free Cash Flow shows how much cash a company generates after paying to maintain and 
expand its production. It is calculated by subtracting Capital Expenditure (Cap Ex) from Cash Flow.

Funds from operations (FFO): This refers to the figure used by real estate investment trusts (REITs) to define the 
cash flow from their operations. It is calculated by adding depreciation and amortization to earnings, subtracting any 
gains on sales. Many leading REITs believe that the FFO calculation is the best presentation of operating results. 
This is because real estate historically rises and falls with macroeconomic conditions, and any operating results 
calculated using the cost accounting method are not an accurate measure of performance.

OCF: Operating cash flow. The measure of cash into or out of the company, similar to Net Income but less easily 
manipulated and without the effects of depreciation and other non-cash charges.

Payout Ratio: Dividend payout ratio is Dividend Per Share as a percent of Diluted Earnings Per Share and can be 
used to measure the chance of a dividend increase or cut. For example, a company with a small Payout Ratio has 
room to increase its dividend.

ROIC: ROIC, or Return on Invested Capital, quantifies how well a company generates cash flow relative to the capital 
it has invested in its business. It is defined as Net Income / (Total Equity + Long-term Debt and Capital Lease Obliga-
tion + Short-term Debt and Capital Lease Obligation)

Shareholder yield: Shareholder yield is the total of share buybacks and dividend payments to common shareholders 
over the past twelve months as a percent of the current market capitalization. A negative value indicates the compa-
ny is profiting more from issuing new stock than it is spending on buybacks and dividends.

TTM: trailing twelve months.

Closing Thoughts

Just glancing at the S&P 500’s chart, it seems evident that the market is looking past this year and toward 2021 
earnings. This may be a bold decision, but the stock market is inherently forward-looking, and with an infinite pool of 
fiscal and monetary stimulus, not to mention the potential for a vaccine or vaccines, it’s not that hard to understand 
where the buyers are coming from. I believe that unless the numbers are more horrific than expected, we may have 
a smooth sailing with very few compelling investment opportunities. That said, some sectors do face challenges 
(retail, financials, and REITs come to mind), so I keep my eyes on the field, and we’ll see where it takes us.

Talking about sectors and investment styles, our strategy of focusing on stocks that have paid rising dividends over 
time has outperformed the market over multiple business cycles with much of that outperformance coming in down 
years (as our approach has tended to prove defensive in risk-off environments). As you must have noticed, this is 
most certainly not the case in 2020 so far. If measured by the basket of Dividend Aristocrats (NOBL) vs. the S&P 
500, dividend investing is lagging in the down market in 2020. I read a piece the other day that elaborated on how 
the tech underweight and the exclusion of Amazon together contributed two-thirds of the performance differential 
year-to-date. I hasten to add that the FALCON Method uses a multifaceted approach (the components of which are 
all proven) as outlined here, so our strategy does differ from simply buying the Dividend Aristocrats regardless of 
their valuation. Still, there is an important point I want to make with this comparison, so bear with me.

As one of SocGen’s strategists highlighted, the massive outperformance of the S&P versus the MSCI rest of the 
world (RoW) is almost entirely attributable to six stocks: Facebook, Apple, Alphabet, Amazon, Netflix, and Microsoft. 
The S&P 494 (ex FAAANM) is just about as dull as the RoW. As Albert Edwards put it, “the pedestrian price perfor-
mance of the S&P 494 in line with the RoW stock markets is entirely in line with both their sales and profits perfor-
mance. Remove the FAAANM stocks, and profits are back to 2015 levels.” Anybody telling me that I’m not beating 
the market (as defined by the S&P 500) must be aware that the performance of this widely accepted benchmark 
is coming from a small group of six stocks. While those six are not your typical dividend stocks (and are thus un-
derrepresented in dividend-focused portfolios), I am ready to admit that most of them are wonderful companies; it’s 
just their valuation that doesn’t make much sense for a conservative value investor like me.

Okay, the stay-at-home nature of this coronavirus-induced recession has given these darlings (and tech stock in 
general) a whole new impetus, but it would be a mistake to overlook the fact that some of their business models 
are ad-revenue driven. It’s hard to imagine who would need or pay for advertising in a world where only these tech 
megacaps survive and where most small and medium businesses have collapsed, yet valuations seem to imply such 
a scenario. All in all, the tech giants are now worth more than the market cap of all the Dividend Aristocrats com-
bined. Of course, you can always join the ranks of those who rush into tech names to ride ‘the megatrend of tech 
outperformance’, but I believe that the natural gravity of the high growth rates underpinning high valuation multiples 
for those companies will work its magic.

Before I started the FALCON Method Newsletter back in 2017, I was investing in stocks purely for dividends, profit, 
and joy. I had no benchmark to beat as I reported to myself. Such an attitude allows one to pick and choose one’s 
battles more carefully. While this is a freedom I wish for everyone, I feel that providing a newsletter service takes that 
away from me, if I let it. My conclusion, to put it bluntly, is that I’m not too eager to go all out to beat the small group 
of today’s tech darlings (i.e., the S&P 500), and I will not lose sleep over underperforming an index that is driven by 
half a dozen ‘not too sensibly priced’ stocks. As I said before, you can always opt for a passive indexing approach, 
or you can jump on the tech bandwagon if that’s your choice. For my own part, my family’s assets will remain 
invested based on the FALCON Method’s evidence-based approach, even at a time when short-term results are less 
than stellar.
